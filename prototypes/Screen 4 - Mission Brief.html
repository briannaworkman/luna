Geological character

CONFIDENCE 87%
The region surrounding Integrity crater sits within the Orientale basin ejecta field, composed primarily of ferroan anorthosite — the ancient crustal material that forms the lunar highlands. Spectral data from LROC WAC[LROC] places this terrain in the same lithological family as samples recovered at Apollo 16, particularly ferroan anorthosite 60025[JSC]. Olivine-bearing lithologies have been tentatively identified in adjacent terrain, suggesting possible mantle exposure from the Orientale impact event[LROC].

Observational record

CONFIDENCE 91%
No Apollo mission reached this longitude. The nearest human-explored terrain is approximately 2,400 km to the east. Prior to Artemis II, the primary surface record consisted of LROC WAC imagery at 100m/px resolution[LROC] and limited spectral data from the Kaguya and Chang'e missions. The Artemis II crew acquired 7,000+ surface images during their April 6 flyby[NASA] — the first direct human observations of this specific terrain. High-resolution LROC NAC imagery at 0.5m/px is available for portions of the region[LROC].

Landing site viability

CONFIDENCE 74%
Solar illumination at 2.66°N 104.92°W is favorable during the projected 2027 Artemis III launch window, with continuous sunlight periods of 8–12 days[NASA SVS]. The far-side location presents significant communications infrastructure requirements — a relay satellite in a halo orbit around the Earth-Moon L2 point would be required for continuous contact. Terrain hazard assessment is incomplete: boulder density and slope data at landing-relevant resolution are not yet available for this specific location. A definitive viability assessment requires full LROC NAC coverage of the proposed landing zone.
